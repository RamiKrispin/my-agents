# LinkedIn Best Practices

Distilled from LinkedIn's own LinkedIn Learning course on content creation
(see source archived at `linkedin_content.md` in the repo root). This is the
source of truth for **what makes content work on LinkedIn**. The example posts
under `examples/<template>/` are the source of truth for **how Rami's voice
sounds**; this file explains *why* the patterns work.

## Why are you posting? (goal + audience)

Before drafting any post, the skill should know — or ask — what goal the post
serves. Common goals (a post should serve **one**, not all):

- **Build reputation as an expert** in a specific area.
- **Teach skills** to a target audience (the next generation, peers).
- **Attract clients** for a course / consultancy / business.
- **Land speaking gigs** at conferences or podcasts.
- **Grow the network** with people who share the focus area.

The audience follows from the goal: industry peers, potential clients, future
employers, or learners. **Don't try to speak to everyone.** A post written for
"everyone in tech" usually reaches no one. A post written for "Python
developers building production AI" reaches that specific audience and earns
saves.

## What works on LinkedIn (and what doesn't)

LinkedIn members engage most with two things:

1. **Posts shared by their connections** — what someone in their network is
   doing, learning, building. Outside Rami's control.
2. **Content grounded in professional knowledge, opinions, or advice from people
   they don't know.** This is the path to reach beyond the network. LinkedIn's
   recommendation system actively connects this kind of content to relevant
   audiences.

The implication: **lean into expertise.** What does Rami uniquely know that
others can't easily write? Docker for ML, production AI patterns, time series
forecasting, course-creation behind-the-scenes. That's what to post.

A useful filter before saving any draft:

> "What unique insight can only Rami write?"

If the answer is "anyone could post this", the post is in the **curate**
bucket — fine in moderation, but not the path to authority.

## The 4 posting strategies

Every post should fit one of these four strategies. They map cleanly to
templates in `examples/`:

### 1. Career insights and advice

- **What it is:** lessons from your career, opinions on workplace topics,
  perspective shaped by working in different companies/cultures/industries.
- **Why it works:** universally relatable on LinkedIn — everyone is trying to
  succeed in their professional life.
- **Prompts to consider:**
  - What do you wish someone had told you earlier in your career?
  - What's your take on hot workplace topics? (4-day workweek, AI in the
    workplace, RTO mandates, job hopping)
  - How has working in different companies, industries, or cultures shaped how
    you show up at work?
- **Maps to:** `lesson` template.

### 2. Industry trends and commentary

- **What it is:** your unique take on news and trends in your space — not just
  the headline, your opinion or insight on it.
- **Why it works:** positions you as a leading voice in a specific field. People
  want **opinions and unique takeaways from experts**, not headlines.
- **Prompts to consider:**
  - What news or trends do your customers / colleagues need to understand to
    make smart decisions today?
  - What are people in your network always asking your perspective on?
- **Where to find trend material:** industry events, publications you follow,
  what other experts in your space are posting, the LinkedIn News module.
- **Maps to:** `opinion`, `contrarian`, `lesson`, or `comparison` template (depending on
  the angle).

### 3. Behind the scenes (BTS)

- **What it is:** the process behind the work — building, teaching, recording,
  shipping. The "you had to be there" view.
- **Why it works:** people follow people, not documentation. BTS humanizes the
  expertise.
- **Examples:** "I rebuilt this Docker environment 30 times while recording the
  course"; "what I learned running this workshop with 80 engineers".
- **Maps to:** `lesson` template.

### 4. Milestones and company news

- **What it is:** new job, partnership, project launch, company earnings, course
  release.
- **Why it works:** real updates from your professional life are inherently
  newsworthy to your network.
- **Critical rule: don't reshare the press release.** Humanize the update.
  - Use **first-person** language.
  - Focus on **why this news matters, and why now**.
  - Acknowledge people who made it possible (and tag them — see tagging rules).
- **Maps to:** `lesson` template (with a milestone framing) or a curation post.

**Don't go all-in on one strategy.** Experiment across all four; check what
resonates with this audience.

### Inspiration source

LinkedIn's **Top Voices program** members (blue badge on profile) are a
selected pool of experts/leaders/innovators consistently producing high-quality
content. Studying their posts is a useful reference for any of the four
strategies.

## The 4 elements of a successful post

Every draft must hit all four:

### Element 1 — A strong hook (first 2 lines)

The single highest-leverage part of a post. Spend the most time here.

- **For text posts:** the first 2 lines are visible in the feed before "see
  more". They earn the click.
- **For video posts:** the first 3 seconds are the equivalent. They earn the
  watch.

Patterns that work:

- **Overarching question** — "Is your resume CRAAP?"
- **Bold but honest opinion** — "Most AI agent failures have nothing to do with
  prompts."
- **Short, title-style caption** — "3 fears every people leader needs to be
  aware of when it comes to feedback."
- **Mini-anecdote** — "I spent 3 hours debugging an AI agent. The root cause
  was a single line in the prompt."

The hook should **signal the value** the reader will get if they keep reading
or watching. **No gimmicks. No clickbait.** The hook is honest about what
follows.

### Element 2 — Deliver value in the body

Two questions to ask while drafting:

1. **How will someone feel more productive or successful after engaging with
   this content?**
2. **Why would someone be interested in this if they don't already know me?**

Tactical rules:

- **Authenticity > authority theatre.** Anyone can use AI to sound expert.
  That's not what builds trust over time. The post needs to sound like Rami,
  with content only Rami is qualified to write.
- **AI tools are fine** for organizing ideas and improving a draft (this skill
  is one of them). The output still needs to sound like Rami.
- **If the topic is long or complex** — split into a series of posts, or use
  formatting to make the structure scannable.
- **Back claims with examples or data** wherever possible.
- **Cite sources** when referencing someone else's content or words.
- **Show, don't tell.** Ground career lessons in specific experiences and
  unique examples — not abstract advice.

### Element 3 — A visual that earns attention

Content has to stand out in a scrollable feed. Even great writing needs a
visual to stop the scroll.

- **"Visual" is broader than "photo".** It includes: photos, infographics,
  charts, **short-form video**.
- **Visuals must support the post's point.** Don't add a stock photo just for
  eyeballs.
- **Short-form video is one of the most effective formats on LinkedIn right
  now.** It does **not** need to be professionally produced — phone, bright or
  quiet space, hit record. The barrier is mostly comfort, not production
  quality.
- **For first-time video creators:** record practice clips that never get
  posted. The goal is comfort on camera, not perfection.
- **Lean on your strengths and interests.** Don't copy what others do — talk
  about what you can talk about.
- **If the visual or video isn't yours: get permission and cite the creator.**

### Element 4 — Invite the audience into the conversation

A great post starts a conversation. That requires actively inviting it.

- **End with a clear call to action.** A question, a prompt, or an invitation
  to share a perspective in the comments.
- **People are slightly intimidated to comment.** A specific, easy-to-answer
  prompt lowers that barrier.
- **Tag people / companies** when they're core to the story you're telling.
  Tag with intention.
- **Don't tag for attention.** Tagging strangers or companies just to "get on
  their radar" is a known anti-pattern; LinkedIn's audience reads it as such.
- **Hashtags** help discovery for collections / campaigns but **are not
  required** for a post to succeed. Rule: **at most 3, at the end of the post**
  (not woven through the body).

## Posting cadence and timing

Two questions: **when**, and **how often**.

### When

- Find the window when **your target audience is online**. For corporate /
  technical audiences, weekday mornings are usually a strong default.
- For audiences split across regions, find the overlap window. (Rami's
  network is mostly EU + US — UK afternoons hit both.)
- **Experiment.** Track what your audience engages with at what time, then
  lean into it.

### How often

- **Minimum: once a week.** Below this, the algorithm doesn't model your
  audience well.
- **Better: a few times a week.** 2-4 posts/week tends to be the sweet spot.
- **Don't post more than once per day.** Two posts in the same window compete
  with each other and split engagement.
- **Consistency beats burst posting.** Week-over-week regularity matters more
  than total post volume.

## Build community through engagement

Posts are not a broadcast channel. The goal is professional relationships
through content.

- **Reply to comments on your own posts** with thoughtful responses — not
  "thanks 🙏" or an emoji that ends the conversation.
- **Comment on others' posts** in your space. Adding your perspective to their
  ideas drives traffic back to your profile and builds new connections, often
  with less effort than an original post.
- **Treat the comment section as the second half of the post.** Long comments
  on others' posts are a legitimate path to grow voice on the platform — Arjun
  in the LinkedIn course confirms this is a low-friction way for people who
  feel intimidated by posting to start.

## LinkedIn News (a discovery and visibility channel)

LinkedIn's in-house editorial team curates trending professionally-relevant
topics — accessible via the LinkedIn News module on the right side of the
homepage, the notifications feed, and the local LinkedIn News page.

Two reasons it matters:

1. **Topic discovery** — what are members in your region/industry actually
   reading and talking about right now.
2. **Visibility boost** — LinkedIn News features member posts that offer
   timely, relevant insights on trending topics. Knowledge-driven content on
   these topics has a higher chance of being featured, which boosts reach.

If a draft is on a trending LinkedIn News topic, that's worth flagging in the
QA notes.

## Profile prerequisite (not the skill's job, but worth knowing)

Engagement on a post drives clicks to the profile. Before posting, the profile
should reflect the story you want to tell. Two relevant settings:

- **Turn on the follow button** in visibility settings — so non-connections can
  follow content without sending a connection request first.
- **Profile copy** should match the post's positioning (the goal/audience above).

This skill doesn't manage the profile, but flag it if the user asks "why isn't
this growing my audience" and the profile is the bottleneck.

## Anti-patterns the skill must refuse

These are derived from the LinkedIn course and from the existing voice guide.
Block them in the QA pass:

- **Reposting a press release** verbatim as a milestone post.
- **Tagging strangers** to get their attention.
- **Hashtag stuffing** — more than 3 tags, or tags woven into the body.
- **Clickbait or gimmick hooks** that don't honestly preview the value.
- **AI-generated voice that doesn't sound like Rami** — content that any
  competent expert could have produced.
- **Visuals unrelated to the post's point.**
- **Engagement bait endings** — "Agree?", "Thoughts?", "What do you think?".
  Use a specific, answerable question instead.
- **Two ideas competing for attention** in one post — split into a series.
- **Posting twice in the same day window** — content competes with itself.

## How the skill uses this file

1. SKILL.md reads this file before drafting any post.
2. The drafter selects **one posting strategy** (career / trends / BTS /
   milestone) based on the source material.
3. The drafter writes for **all four post elements** (hook / value / visual /
   invite to converse).
4. The QA pass checks the **anti-pattern list** above and the offline
   validator (`scripts/validate.py`).
5. If the user asks about cadence ("when should I post this?"), use the
   cadence guidance in this file.
